About Me

A Mining Engineer Who Went Underground

Mining engineering isn't just a profession — it's a commitment to working in some of the most demanding environments on earth. I was drawn to the engineering problems that live deep below the surface: how do you move thousands of tonnes of coal safely, efficiently, and without disrupting the rock mass above you?

My journey began with a CGPA of 9.0 in Class 10 and progressed through a rigorous four-year B.Tech in Mining Engineering at Rungta College, Bhilai — graduating with CGPA 8.12 in the First (Honors) division. I have walked the entries of the Kurja Underground Mine under SECL, deployed fleet-tracking technology at Coal India sites, and earned every statutory certification required of a serious underground mining professional in India.

Each phase has layered my thinking about mining — not just as extraction, but as a system of people, machines, safety, and data working together.

Personal Philosophy

The Mindset That Drives the Work

Skills and qualifications can be measured. What is harder to measure — but ultimately more important — is how someone approaches the work.

🔍
Curiosity First

I'm drawn to the question underneath the question. Not just "why did the machine break down" but "what in our maintenance process allowed it to reach that point." That curiosity is what made me study mining engineering, and what made me want to understand digital fleet systems on top of underground operations.

📐
Precision Without Shortcuts

In a coal mine, a shortcut is not a time-saver — it's a safety risk. I carry that standard into every piece of work. Whether achieving 100% data accuracy on the KiarX deployment or ensuring every gas test at SECL was correctly documented, I don't look for the path of least resistance when precision matters.

🌱
Always Learning

I went from classroom theory to underground operations to digital mine monitoring in three years. Each transition required learning an entirely new domain quickly and applying it under real conditions. I don't wait to feel fully ready before taking on new responsibility — I learn by doing, and I iterate.

🤝
Ownership Over Output

I take personal responsibility for outcomes. Whether leading the BKT Minetech deployment independently within three months or managing full event coordination as a college volunteer with no template to follow — I find it more natural to own a problem than to wait for someone else to solve it.

Looking Forward

Where I'm Headed

Mining in India is at a crossroads. The demand for coal is not disappearing — it is the backbone of the country's energy grid — but the industry is under real pressure to produce more safely, more efficiently, and with better visibility into what is actually happening underground.

I want to be an engineer who helps solve that problem. Deep domain knowledge plus technology fluency — that combination is rare in Indian mining, and it is what I am building toward.
